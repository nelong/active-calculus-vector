    Section 10.4 The TNB Frame

    Motivating Questions

    • How can we measure the direction of travel on a parametrized curve?
    • How can we measure the direction of turning on a parameterized curve?
    • How can we measure the axis of rotation along a parameterized curve?
    In first section, we saw how vvf of 1 variable graphically cooresponds to a curve in space. While the derivative, integral, and limits apply to these functions componentwise, we will need to apply our calculus and vector tools together to measure some important properties of these curves. Conceptually, you may want to visualize the parameterization of the curve in space as a way to travel along those points in space. Remember that parameterizations are not unique; There is more than one way to walk the same path.
    For example, consider the curve in space that we want to study looks like this (show racetrack plot) and is actually a race track. Not every driver will travel along this curve in the same way, but every driver has to go through these same points in space (everyone will stay on the track and not take shortcuts). Some properties you measure will depend on the particular way that a driver goes along the course and some properties will be the same for all drivers (when measured at the same location on the track).

    Subsection 10.4.1 Direction of travel

    T is the direction of travel. Should make sense from our discussion of the velocity vector that \(/vv(t)=\vr'(t)\) is in the direction of travel. Since speed is not 1 we define the direction of travel as follows.

    Definition 10.4.2.

    The unit tangent vector to a curve is denoted by \(\vec{T}(t)\) is computed by
    \begin{equation*} \vec{T}(t)=\frac{\vv(t)}{\Vert{\vv(t)}\Vert} \end{equation*}
    The unit tangent vector measures the direction of travel along a parameterized curve.
    The unit tangent exists as long as the velocity exists and is not the zero vector. This should make sense because if there is not motion or there is jump/discontinuity in the derivative, the direction of travel at that instant does not make sense.
    The definition of T has allowed us to separate the velocity into its magnitude (speed) and direction (unit tangent). Remember that T changes along the curve and is calculated as a function of the parameter.

    Recalling our analogy of our parameterization describing how we are driving along a racetrack, T measures the direction that that car is heading in at any given moment. Look at t=2 on ounr track, we see that T(2)= Show a plot with all of this. What direction is the car turning at t=2? you can see by the plot that the car will be be turning right/left, but that is a relative direction to the car. So how can we measure the direction that the car is turning at this instant, in terms of the larger coordinate system?
    Let’s look at \(\vec{T}'=\frac{d\vec{T}}{dt}\text{,}\) which will measure how quickly \(\vec{T}\) is changing in terms of the parameter. Remember that vectors have magnitude and direction, so the derivative of a vector valued function of 1 variable can measure the change in the magnitude and a change in the direction. Since T will always have length 1 (when T exists), all of the change in T will correspond to a change in direction. So the direction of \(\vec{T}'=\frac{d\vec{T}}{dt}\) will be exactly what we are looking for; namely, \(\vec{T}'=\frac{d\vec{T}}{dt}\) will be in the direction of turning. We don’t care (for now) about how fast the car is turning, just what direciton that turning is in. So we define N as the unit vector in the direction of \(\vec{T}'=\frac{d\vec{T}}{dt}\text{.}\)

    Definition 10.4.4.

    The unit normal vector to a curve is denoted by \(\vec{N}(t)\) is computed by
    \begin{equation*} \vec{N}(t)=\frac{\vec{T}'(t)}{\Vert{\vec{T}'(t)}} \end{equation*}
    The unit norml vector measures the direction of turning along a parameterized curve.

    Subsection 10.4.2 The Binormal Vector

    Definition 10.4.6.

    The Binormal vector, \(\vec{B}\text{,}\) for a parameterized curve is defined as
    \begin{equation*} \vec{B}=\vec{T} \times \vec{N} \end{equation*}
    This definition of B creates a three dimensional right handed coordinate system that is relative to the motion along the curve. B will be a unit vector because \(\Vert \vT \times \vN \Vert = \Vert \vT\Vert \Vert \vN \Vert \sin(theta)\) where theta is the angle between \(\vT\) and \(\vN\text{.}\) Since \(\vT\) and \(vN\) are normal vectors and orthogonal to each other, then \(\Vert \vT\Vert \Vert \vN \Vert \sin(theta)= 1\text{.}\) B will give the axis of rotation for the motion along the curve. Note that this measure of rotation follows our right handed measure of rotation. If you put the fingers of your right hand in the direction of travel (\(\vT\)) and curl your fingers in the direction of turning (\(\vN\)), then your thumb will correspond to the axis of rotation based on how you are moving along the curve.
    This TNB frame is sometimes called the Frenet frame and is very useful in environments like aviation and space travel because so much of the relevent information needs be in terms of relative measurements to the craft and it’s motion.

    Subsection 10.4.3 The Driver or the Road: TNB Frame

    In this subsection, we will talk about whether the measurements of T, N, and B will be properties of the driver or the road. A parameterized curve also has a stated orientation (direction of travel). Remember that a measurement involving a parameterized curve is a property of the driver if two different parameterizations can have the different measurements at the same location on the curve. Remember that this does not mean that you look at the same parameter value on the curve, but rather the same location on the curve. A measurement on a parametertized curve is a property of the road if every parameterization of this curve must have the same value(s) for a location on the curve.

    Subsection 10.4.5 Summary

    • The integration process shows that the length \(L\) of a smooth curve defined by \(\vr(t)\) on an interval \([a,b]\) is
      \begin{equation*} L = \int_a^b |\vr'(t)| \, dt. \end{equation*}
    • Arc length is useful as a parameter because when we parameterize with respect to arc length, we eliminate the role of speed in our calculation of curvature and the result is a measure that depends only on the geometry of the curve and not on the parameterization of the curve.
    • We define the curvature \(\kappa\) of a curve in 2- or 3-space to be the rate of change of the magnitude of the unit tangent vector with respect to arc length, or
      \begin{equation*} \kappa = \left\lvert \frac{d\vT}{ds} \right\rvert. \end{equation*}

    3.

    Consider the moving particle whose position at time \(t\) in seconds is given by the vector-valued function \(\vr\) defined by \(\vr(t) = 5t \vi + 4\sin(3t) \vj + 4\cos(3t) \vk\text{.}\) Use this function to answer each of the following questions.
    1. Find the unit tangent vector, \(\vT(t)\text{,}\) to the space curve traced by \(\vr(t)\) at time \(t\text{.}\) Write one sentence that explains what \(\vT(t)\) tells us about the particle’s motion.
    2. Determine the speed of the particle moving along the space curve with the given parameterization.
    3. Find the exact distance traveled by the particle on the time interval \([0,\pi/3]\text{.}\)
    4. Find the average velocity of the particle on the time interval \([0, \pi/3]\text{.}\)
    5. Determine the parameterization of the given curve with respect to arc length.
    Solution.
    1. The unit tangent vector \(\vT(t)\) is found as
      \begin{equation*} \vT(t) = \frac{\vr'(t)}{|\vr'(t)|} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{13}}(5 \vi + 12\cos(3t) \vj - 12\sin(3t) \vk)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      The unit tangent vector \(\vT(t)\) tells us the direction of motion of the particle at time \(t\text{.}\)
    2. The speed of the particle is the magnitude of the velocity vector, so the speed of the particle at time \(t\) is
      \begin{equation*} |\vr'(t)| = 13\text{.} \end{equation*}
      So the particle is moving at a constant speed.
    3. The distance traveled by the particle on a time interval \([a,b]\) is the length of the arc on \([a,b]\text{.}\) In this case, the exact distance traveled by the particle on the time interval \([0,\pi/3]\) is
      \begin{equation*} \int_0^{\pi/3} |\vr'(t)| \, dt = \int_0^{\pi/3} 13 \, dt = \frac{13 \pi}{3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    4. The average velocity of the particle on the time interval \([0, \pi/3]\) is
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\pi/3} (\vr(\pi/3) - \vr(0)) = \frac{3}{\pi} \left\langle \frac{5 \pi}{3}, 0, -8 \right\rangle\text{.} \end{equation*}
    5. The arclength from \(0\) to \(t\) is
      \begin{equation*} s = L(t) = \int_0^t |\vr'(w)| \, dw = 13 t\text{,} \end{equation*}
      so \(t = L^{-1}(s) = \frac{1}{13}s\text{.}\) Therefore, a parameterization of the curve with respect to arc length is
      \begin{equation*} \vr(s) = \vr\left(\frac{1}{13}s\right) = \frac{5}{13}s \vi + 4\sin\left(\frac{3}{13}s\right) \vj + 4\cos\left(\frac{3}{13}s\right) \vk\text{.} \end{equation*}

    1. Recall that the unit tangent vector, \(\vT(t)\text{,}\) is the vector tangent to the curve at time \(t\) that points in the direction of motion and has length 1. Find \(\vT(t)\text{.}\)
    2. Explain why the fact that \(| \vT(t) | = 1\) implies that \(\vT\) and \(\vT'\) are orthogonal vectors for every value of \(t\text{.}\) (Hint: note that \(\vT \cdot \vT = |\vT|^2 = 1,\) and compute \(\frac{d}{dt}[\vT \cdot \vT]\text{.}\))
    3. For the given function \(\vr\) with unit tangent vector \(\vT(t)\) (from (a)), determine \(\vN(t) = \frac{1}{|\vT'(t)|} \vT'(t)\text{.}\)
    4. What geometric properties does \(\vN(t)\) have? That is, how long is this vector, and how is it situated in comparison to \(\vT(t)\text{?}\)
    5. Let \(\vB(t) = \vT(t) \times \vN(t)\text{,}\) and compute \(\vB(t)\) in terms of your results in (a) and (c).
    6. What geometric properties does \(\vB(t)\) have? That is, how long is this vector, and how is it situated in comparison to \(\vT(t)\) and \(\vN(t)\text{?}\)
    7. Sketch a plot of the given helix, and compute and sketch \(\vT(\pi/2)\text{,}\) \(\vN(\pi/2)\text{,}\) and \(\vB(\pi/2)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. Here we have
      \begin{equation*} \vr'(t) = (-\sin(t)) \vi + \cos(t) \vj + \vk \end{equation*}
      and
      \begin{equation*} \vT(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left( (-\sin(t)) \vi + \cos(t) \vj + \vk \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. Note that
      \begin{equation*} \lvert \vT(t) \rvert = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\sqrt{\sin^2(t) + \cos^2(t) + 1} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
      So
      \begin{align*} 0 \amp = \frac{d}{dt} (1)\\ \amp = \frac{d}{dt}[\vT \cdot \vT]\\ \amp = (\vT(t) \cdot \vT'(t)) + (\vT'(t) \cdot \vT(t))\\ \amp = 2(\vT(t) \cdot \vT'(t))\text{.} \end{align*}
      So \(\vT(t) \cdot \vT'(t) = 0\) for every value of \(t\) and \(\vT\) and \(\vT'\) are orthogonal vectors for every value of \(t\text{.}\)
    3. With \(\vT(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left( (-\sin(t)) \vi + \cos(t) \vj + \vk \right)\) we have
      \begin{equation*} \vT'(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left( (-\cos(t)) \vi - \sin(t) \vj \right) \text{ and } |\vT'(t)| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      So
      \begin{equation*} \vN(t) = \frac{1}{|\vT'(t)|} \vT'(t) = -(\cos(t) \vi + \sin(t) \vj)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    4. The vector \(\vN(t)\) is a unit vector. Since \(\vT(t) \cdot \vN(t) = 0\text{,}\) the two vectors \(\vT(t)\) and \(\vN(t)\) are orthogonal for every value of \(t\text{.}\)
    5. Here we have
      \begin{equation*} \vB(t) = \vT(t) \times \vN(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left( (-\sin(t)) \vi + \cos(t) \vj + \vk \right) \times [-(\cos(t) \vi + \sin(t) \vj)] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (\sin(t) \vi - \cos(t) \vj + \vk)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    6. The vector \(\vB(t)\) is a unit vector, and
      \begin{equation*} \vB(t) \cdot \vT(t) = 0, \ \ \vB(t) \cdot \vN(t) = 0 \end{equation*}
      shows that \(\vB(t)\) is orthogonal to both \(\vT(t)\) and \(\vN(t)\) for every value of \(t\text{.}\) The vector \(\vN(t)\) is called the principal unit normal vector to the curve and the vector \(\vB(t)\) is the binormal vector. These three vectors,
      \begin{equation*} \vT(t) = \frac{\vr'(t)}{\lvert \vr'(t) \rvert}, \ \ \vN(t) = \frac{1}{\lvert \vT'(t) \rvert} \vT'(t), \ \ \text{ and } \ \ \vB(t) = \vT(t) \times \vN(t) \end{equation*}
      are always unit vectors orthogonal to each other. The plane determined by the vectors \(\vN(t)\) and \(\vB(t)\) is called the normal plane to the curve at \(t\) and consists of all lines that are orthogonal to the tangent line to the curve at \(t\text{.}\) The plane determined by \(\vT(t)\) and \(\vN(t)\) is called the osculating plane and is the plane the comes closest to containing the curve for inputs near \(t\text{.}\) There is a circle that lies in the osculating plane that has the same tangent as the curve at \(t\text{,}\) lies on the concave side of the curve (the direction indicated by \(\vN(t)\)), and has radius \(\frac{1}{\kappa(t)}\text{.}\) This circle is called the osculating circle and it is the circle that best describes how the curve behaves for inputs near \(t\) in the sense that this circle has the same tangent and normal vectors at \(t\) and has the came curvature at that point as the curve.
    7. Evaluating \(\vT(t)\text{,}\) \(\vN(t)\text{,}\) and \(\vB(t)\) at \(t = \frac{\pi}{2}\) yields
      \begin{equation*} \vT \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\langle -1,0,1 \rangle, \ \vN \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \langle 0,-1,0 \rangle, \ \text{ and } \ \vB \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\langle 1,0,1 \rangle\text{.} \end{equation*}
