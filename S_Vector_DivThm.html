    Section 12.12 The Divergence Theorem

    Motivating Questions

    • What is a closed surface in \(\R^3\text{?}\)
    • How can we efficiently calculate the flux through a closed surface in \(\R^3\) when that surface must be parametrized in several pieces?
    In Section¬†12.6 we examined vector fields for how the strength of a vector field changes in different regions. In particular, we developed the divergence of a vector field as a local measurement (based on density) of how the strength of the vector field changes. In particular, we did this by looking at the flux of the vector field through a closed path in two dimensions, then generalizing these ideas to higher dimensions.
    In Section¬†12.9, we measured how much of a vector field flowed through a section of a surface in three dimensions as a generalization of our argument from Section¬†12.6. In this section, we will connect the ideas of flux of a vector field through a closed surface in three dimensions and the divergence of that vector field.
    A surface in three dimensions is said to be closed if it has no boundary. Recalling the surfaces we studied in Section¬†12.11 where we applied Stokes‚Äô Theorem, none of those surfaces were closed because the boundary of the surface through which we calculated the flux was the curve along which we calculated the circulation. In Figure¬†12.12.1, we show two surfaces that are not closed, as demonstrated by the red curves marking the edge where the surface ends.
    In fact, the yellow cylinder has two edges where the surface ends (does not meet itself). The surfaces in Figure¬†12.12.2 are closed because there is no edge to the surface. Note that the cylinder has been ‚Äúfilled in‚Äù with a cap at top and bottom (plotted in gray and green, respectively) to become a closed surface.
    Closed surfaces can be used to define the boundary of a volume in space. If we have the top and bottom on our cylinder, we have a well-defined volume of space, in that we know which points are ‚Äúinside‚Äù the volume and which are ‚Äúoutside‚Äù of the volume. With different top and bottom surfaces, the enclosed volume would be different. Figure¬†12.12.3 illustrates three different ways to complete the cylindrical surface into a closed surface.

The net flux through the closed surface measures the net amount of the vector field that is created or destroyed on the interior of the closed surface.

    Subsection 12.12.2 The Divergence Theorem

    The divergence of a vector field was developed as a measurement of the density with which the strength of vector field is changing. In three dimensions, the divergence measures the volume density in which the vector field is being created or destroyed. This means that if we integrate the divergence of a vector field over a volume of space, we will get the net amount of the vector field that is created or destroyed in that particular volume of space. Since the net amount of a vector field that is created or destroyed in a volume of space is the same as the net flux of the vector field through the boundary of that volume (which is a closed surface), we have a correspondence between a triple integral of the divergence of a vector field on the interior of a closed surface and the flux integral of the vector field over the closed surface.

    Theorem 12.12.7. Divergence Theorem.

    Let \(S\) be a closed surface in three dimensional space and let \(Q\) be the volume bounded by \(S\text{.}\) If \(\vF\) is a vector field that has continuous first partial derivatives on \(Q\) and \(S\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \iint_{S} \vF \cdot \vN\, dS = \iiint_Q \divg(\vF)\, dV \end{equation*}
    where the integral on the left measures the flux through \(S\) in terms of the outward pointing normal vector.
    The preview activity and the discussion before the statement of the Divergence Theorem have hopefully given you some intuition as to why the theorem is true. The ideas should also seem similar to the manner in which we approached Green‚Äôs Theorem and Theorem¬†12.11.5. In the next example, we will verify the Divergence Theorem for a particular case.

    Example 12.12.8.

    In this example we will apply the Divergence Theorem to the vector field \(\vF=\langle xy-z, yz+e^x,z(x-y) \rangle \) and the region in the first octant bounded above by \(z=1-x-y\text{.}\) We will go through the calculations of the flux integral on the right side and the triple integral on the left side.
    (a)
    In this task, we will calculate the left side of the Divergence Theorem using four flux integrals (for each of the four boundary surfaces) as drawn in Figure¬†12.12.9. We will need to parametrize each of the four faces which we will call \(S_1\) (in red), \(S_2\) (in yellow), \(S_3\) (in blue), and \(S_4\) (in green).
    • \(\displaystyle \vr_1(s,t)= \langle 0,s,t \rangle\)
    • \(\displaystyle \vr_2(s,t)= \langle s,0,t \rangle\)
    • \(\displaystyle \vr_3(s,t)= \langle s,t,0 \rangle\)
    • \(\displaystyle \vr_4(s,t)= \langle s,t,1-s-t \rangle\)
    where all of these parameterization use \(0\leq s \leq 1\) and \(0 \leq t \leq 1-s\text{.}\) Each of these parameterizations gives the following normal vectors:
    • \(\displaystyle \vn_1 = \langle 1,0,0 \rangle\)
    • \(\displaystyle \vn_2 = \langle 0,-1,0 \rangle\)
    • \(\displaystyle \vn_3 = \langle 0,0,1 \rangle\)
    • \(\displaystyle \vn_4 = \langle 1,1,1 \rangle\)
    Notice that \(\vn_1\) and \(\vn_3\) point into \(Q\) while \(\vn_2\) and \(\vn_4\) points out of \(Q\text{.}\) We will take into account the idea that we will need to calculate the flow out of \(Q\) when we sum our flux integrals later.
    (b)
    In this task, we will calculate the triple integral on the right side of the Divergence Theorem for our example. Since \(\vF=\langle xy-z, yz+e^x,z(x-y) \rangle \text{,}\) we can calculate \(\divg(\vF)= \dfrac{\partial}{\partial x}(xy-z)+\dfrac{\partial}{\partial y}(yz-e^x)+\dfrac{\partial}{\partial z}(z(x-y)) = y+z+(x-y)\text{.}\) The region \(Q\) can be described by the bounds \(0\leq x\leq 1\text{,}\) \(0\leq y\leq 1-x\text{,}\) and \(0\leq z\leq 1-x-y\text{.}\) Thus, our triple integral will be setup and evaluated by
    \begin{gather*} \int_0^1 \int_0^{1-x} \int_0^{1-x-y} y+z+x \enspace dz \enspace dy \enspace dx \\ \int_0^1 \int_0^{1-x} y(1-x-y)+1/2(1-x-y)+x(1-x-y) \enspace dy \enspace dx \\ \int_0^1 \frac{1}{12} (4x^3-3x^2-6x+5) \enspace dx \end{gather*}
    which will evaluate to \(\frac{1}{12}\text{.}\) This matches with the flux calculations we did in the previous task to verify the Divergence Theorem for our example.

    Subsection 12.12.3 Summary

    • A closed surface is one that has no boundary.
    • The flux of a smooth vector field through a closed surface can be computed by integrating the divergence of the vector field over the volume bounded by the closed surface.

    Exercises 12.12.4 Exercises

    1.

    Verify the Divergence Theorem for the vector field and region:
    \(\mathbf{F} = \left\lt 5x ,8z ,5y \right>\) and the region \(x^2+y^2\le 1\text{,}\) \(0\le z \le 8\)
    \(\iint_{\mathcal{S}} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} =\)
    \(\iiint_{\mathcal{R}} \text{div} (\mathbf{F}) \,dV=\)
    Solution.
    With \(z_0=8\) Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the surface of the cylinder and \(\mathcal{R}\) the region enclosed by \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) We compute the two sides of the Divergence Theorem:
    \begin{equation*} \iint_{\mathcal{S}} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_{\mathcal{R}} \text{div} (\mathbf{F}) \,dV \end{equation*}
    We first calculate the surface integral. Step 1. Integral over the side of the cylinder. The side of the cylinder is parametrized by
    \begin{equation*} \Phi (\theta ,z)=(\cos \theta , \sin \theta ,z),\quad 0\le \theta \le 2\pi ,\quad 0\le z\le 8 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{n} = \left\lt \cos \theta , \sin \theta ,0 \right> \end{equation*}
    Then,
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{F} \left(\Phi (\theta ,z)\right)\cdot \mathbf{n} = \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \left\lt 5 \cos \theta ,8 z,5\sin \theta \right> \cdot \left\lt \cos \theta , \sin \theta ,0 \right>= \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 5 \cos^2\theta +8 z \sin \theta \end{equation*}
    We obtain the integral
    \begin{equation*} \iint_{\mathrm{side}} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \int_0^{8} \int_0^{2\pi } \left(5 \cos^2\theta +8 z \sin \theta \right) \,d\theta \,dz= \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 40 \int_0^{2\pi } \cos^2\theta \,d\theta +\left(\int_0^{8} 8 z \,dz\right)\left(\int_0^{2\pi } \sin \theta \,d\theta \right)= \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 40\cdot \left(\frac{\theta }{2}+\frac{\sin 2\theta }{4}\bigg|_0^{2\pi }\right)+0= 40\pi \end{equation*}
    Step 2. Integral over the top of the cylinder. The top of the cylinder is parametrized by
    \begin{equation*} \Phi(x,y)=(x,y,8) \end{equation*}
    with parameter domain \(\mathcal{D}=\lbrace (x,y):\, x^2+ y^2\le 1\rbrace\text{.}\) The upward pointing normal is
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{n} ={\mathbf{T} }_x \times {\mathbf{T} }_y = \left\lt 1,0,0 \right>\times \left\lt 0,1,0 \right> = \end{equation*}

    Step 3. Integral over the bottom of the cylinder. We parametrize the bottom by

    \begin{equation*} 40\pi +0+0=40\pi \quad\mathbf{(1)} \end{equation*}
    Step 4. Compare with integral of divergence.
    \begin{equation*} \text{div} (\mathbf{F} ) = \text{div} \left\lt 5 x,8 z,5 y \right>= \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial }{\partial x}(5 x)+\frac{\partial }{\partial y}(8 z)+\frac{\partial }{\partial z}(5 y)=5 \end{equation*}

    The equality of \(\mathbf{(1)}\) and \(\mathbf{(2)}\) verifies the Divergence Theorem.
