Example 12.5.14.

Let \(C_1\) be part of the unit circle on the \(xy\)-plane that is centered at the origin and is above the line given by \(y=-x\text{.}\) We will also consider \(f_3(x,y,z)=x-y\text{.}\) We can parameterize \(C_1\) by \(\vr(t)=\langle \cos(t), \sin(t), 0 \rangle\) with \(-\frac{\pi}{4}\leq t\leq \frac{3\pi}{4}]\text{.}\) Note here that \(|{\vr'(t)}| =|{\langle -\sin(t),\cos(t),0\rangle}|=1\text{.}\) We can write the value of \(f_3\) along \(C_1\) using the parameterization as \(f_3(x(t),y(t),z(t)) = f_3(\cos(t),\sin(t),0) = \cos(t)-\sin(t)\text{.}\) Applying Theorem 12.5.11, we have
\begin{align*} \int_{C_1} f_3 \, ds \amp = \int_{-\frac{\pi}{4}}^{\frac{3\pi}{4}}\left(\cos(t)-\sin(t)\right) (1) dt\\ \amp = \sin(\frac{3\pi}{4})-\cos(\frac{3\pi}{4}) - \left(\sin(-\frac{\pi}{4})-\cos(-\frac{\pi}{4})\right)=0 \end{align*}
This result should make sense from your earlier work in part 12.5.2.b. Specifically, we can break the line integral of \(f_3\) into two parts according to the plot in Figure 12.5.15.
The scalar line integal on the blue and the magenta paths will cancel each other out exactly since the paths are symmetric and the output of \(f_3\) will be opposite in sign on the blue and magenta. Thus the line integral of \(f_3\) over \(C_1\) will be zero.
