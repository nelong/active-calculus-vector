Example 12.10.8.

Let us return to some of the problems from Activity 12.10.2. Specifically, we will compute the answer to Task 12.10.2.b. We will compute \(\iint_{S_1} x \enspace dS\) where \(S_1\) is the top half (\(z \geq 0\)) of the sphere of radius one centered at the origin in 3-space. Our surface can be parameterized by \(\vr(s,t)=\langle \cos(s)\sin(t),\sin(s)\sin(t),\cos(t)\rangle\) where \(s \in [0,2\pi]\) and \(t \in [0,\pi]\text{.}\) Using Theorem 12.10.4, we need to compute the partial derivatives of \(\vr\) with respect to \(s\) and \(t\text{,}\) then compute \(\Vert{\vr_s \times \vr_t}\Vert \text{.}\) For our parameterization, we get
\begin{align*} \vr_s \amp = \langle -\sin(s)\sin(t),\cos(s)\sin(t),0\rangle \\ \vr_t \amp = \langle \cos(s)\cos(t),\sin(s)\cos(t),-\sin(t)\rangle \\ \vr_s \times \vr_t \amp = \langle -(\sin(t))^2 \cos(s), \\ \amp \quad \quad - (\sin(t))^2 \sin(s), -(\cos(s))^2 \cos(t) \sin(t) -(\sin(s))^2 \cos(t) \sin(t) \rangle \end{align*}
Simplifying \(\vr_s \times \vr_t\) we get
\begin{equation*} \vr_s \times \vr_t =\sin(t) \langle \cos(s)\sin(t),\sin(s)\sin(t),\cos(t) \rangle \end{equation*}
Note that \(\vr_s \times \vr_t = \sin(t) \vr(s,t)\text{.}\) This should make sense because the normal vector to a sphere should be the outward pointing vector from the center of the sphere. Note here that \(\Vert{\vr_s \times \vr_t}\Vert = \sin(t)\) because \(\Vert{\vr(s,t)}\Vert = 1\) for any \(s\) or \(t\text{.}\)
We can now use Theorem 12.10.4 to compute the scalar surface integral as
\begin{gather*} \iint_{S_1} x \enspace dS = \int_0^{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} \cos(s)\sin(t) (\sin(t)) \enspace ds dt\\ = \int_0^{\pi}(\sin(t))^2 dt [\sin(s)]|_{0}^{2\pi} = \int_0^{\pi}(\sin(t))^2 dt [0] =0 \end{gather*}
This result should match your answer for Task 12.10.2.b since the output of the function \(f(x,y,z)=x\) would be symmetric but opposite in sign for the parts of the surface in octants 1 and 4 as compared to octants 2 and 3.
