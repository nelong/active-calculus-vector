Example 12.10.9.

Let us return to one of the problems from Activity 12.10.2. Specifically, we will compute the answer to part a of that activity. We will compute \(\iint_{S_1} x \, dS\) where \(S_1\) is the top half (\(z \geq 0\)) of the sphere of radius one centered at the origin. Thinking in terms of spherical coordinates is helpul for determining a parameterization of \(S_1\text{.}\) Here we can use \(\vr(s,t)=\langle \cos(s)\sin(t),\sin(s)\sin(t),\cos(t)\rangle\) where \(0 \leq s\leq 2\pi\) and \(0\leq t\leq \pi/2\text{,}\) which comes from thinking of \(s=\theta\) and \(t=\phi\) in spherical coordinates (and \(\rho=1\)). Using Theorem 12.10.4, we need to compute the partial derivatives of \(\vr\) with respect to \(s\) and \(t\text{,}\) then compute \(|{\vr_s \times \vr_t}| \text{.}\) For our parameterization, we get
\begin{align*} \vr_s \amp = \langle -\sin(s)\sin(t),\cos(s)\sin(t),0\rangle \\ \vr_t \amp = \langle \cos(s)\cos(t),\sin(s)\cos(t),-\sin(t)\rangle \\ \vr_s \times \vr_t \amp = -\sin(t) \langle \cos(s)\sin(t),\sin(s)\sin(t),\cos(t) \rangle \end{align*}
The form given for \(\vr_s \times \vr_t\) is simplified. Before simplifying, we have
\begin{align*} \langle \amp -(\sin(t))^2 \cos(s), -(\sin(t))^2 \sin(s),\\ \amp -(\cos(s))^2 \cos(t) \sin(t) -(\sin(s))^2 \cos(t) \sin(t) \rangle\text{.} \end{align*}
Note that \(\vr_s \times \vr_t = -\sin(t) \vr(s,t)\text{,}\) which means that \(\vecmag{\vr_s \times \vr_t} = \sin(t)\) because \(\vecmag{\vr(s,t)} = 1\) for any \(s\) or \(t\) and \(\sin(t)\geq 0\) for \(0\leq t\leq \pi/2\text{.}\)
We can now use Theorem 12.10.4 to compute the scalar surface integral as
\begin{align*} \iint_{S_1} x \, dS \amp = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{2\pi} \left[\cos(s)\sin(t)\right] (\sin(t)) \, ds\, dt\\ \amp = \left[\sin(s)\right]_{0}^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}(\sin(t))^2\, dt = 0\cdot \int_0^{\pi}(\sin(t))^2\, dt =0\text{.} \end{align*}
This result should match your answer for Task a of Activity 12.10.2 since the values of the function \(f(x,y,z)=x\) are symmetric but opposite in sign for the parts of the surface in the octants with \(x\gt 0\) as compared to the octants with \(x\lt 0\text{.}\)
