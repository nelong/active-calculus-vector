Activity 12.3.2.

In this activity, we will look at a process to write a line integral of a line segment as a definite integral in one variable. This uses the same set up as the Preview Activity including Figure 12.3.1. In particular, evaluating \(\int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\) takes more work at this stage, so let’s break the process into a few steps.

(a)

Find the slope-intercept (\(y=mx+b\)) form of the equation of the line containing the line segment \(C_3\text{.}\)
Solution.

(b)

We now know how to express \(y\) in terms of \(x\) for all points along \(C_3\text{.}\) Use this to express the components of \(\vF\) in terms of only \(x\) for the points along \(C_3\text{.}\)
Solution.

(c)

We often think of the slope of a line as being \(\Delta y/\Delta x\text{.}\) Use this fact and the slope of the line containing the segment \(C_3\) to express \(\Delta y\) as a multiple of \(\Delta x\text{.}\)
Solution.

(d)

We may view \(\Delta\vr\) as \(\langle \Delta x,\Delta y\rangle\text{.}\) Since \(\Delta x\approx dx\) and \(\Delta y\approx dy\text{,}\) write \(d\vr\) as a vector in terms of \(dx\text{.}\) You should be able to factor out \(dx\) from your result and write your answer in the form \(\langle a_1,a_2 \rangle\, dx\text{.}\)
Solution.

(e)

Use the range of \(x\)-values covered by the line segment \(C_3\) to write \(\int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\) as a single-variable integral of the form \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\) and evaluate the integral.
Solution.

(f)

Notice that \(C\) and \(C_3\) both start at \((1,1)\) and end at \((4,3)\text{.}\) How do the values of \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr\) and \(\int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\) compare?
Solution.

(g)

Is \(\vF\) a gradient vector field? Why or why not?
