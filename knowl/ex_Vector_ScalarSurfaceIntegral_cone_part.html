Example 12.10.5.

For this example, we will consider the surface \(S_1\) given by \(x^2+y^2=z^2\) such that \(1\leq z \leq 3\text{.}\) This is the same part of the cone used in Example 12.9.8. We also let \(f(x,y,z)=x\text{.}\)
(a)
For our first part of our example, we use symmetry to reason about \(\iint_{S_1} f\, dS\text{.}\) Without using any calculations or parameterizations, we can reason that this scalar surface integral will be zero. In a scalar surface integral, we are calculating the accumulation of the scalar output over a surface area in space. In Figure 12.10.6, you can see that we can symmetrically break our surface \(S_1\) into two regions that have exactly the same surface area properties and symmetric output values for our scalar function, \(f\text{.}\) Here we denote by \(S_2\) the portion of \(S_1\) with \(x\geq 0\) (colored green), while \(S_3\) is the magenta portion of the surface, which has \(x \lt 0\text{.}\) The only difference between the green and magenta portions of Figure 12.10.6 that is relevant to our surface integral is that the values of \(f\) will be positive on the green surface \(S_2\) and negative on the magenta surface \(S_3\) No matter how we break up the Riemann sum or the corresponding scalar surface integral for the green side of our surface, applying the same pattern to the magenta side will give the same result but with an opposite sign. In other words,
\begin{equation*} \int_{S_2} f \, dS = - \int_{S_3} f \, dS\text{.} \end{equation*}
Therefore,
\begin{equation*} \int_{S_1} f \, dS = \int_{S_2} f \, dS + \int_{S_3} f \, dS = \int_{S_2} f \, dS - \int_{S_2} f \, dS =0\text{.} \end{equation*}
(b)
From a calculation standpoint, we can verify this result using our parameterization from Example 12.9.8 in Theorem 12.10.4. Specifically, we will use the parameterization of \(S_1\) given by \(\vr(s,t)=\langle s \cos(t), s \sin(t), s \rangle \) with bounds \(1\leq s \leq 3\) and \(0 \leq t \lt 2 \pi\text{.}\) We also know from Example 12.9.8 that
\begin{equation*} \vw= \vr_s \times \vr_t = \langle-s \cos(t), -s \sin(t), s \rangle \text{.} \end{equation*}
Hence, we have \(\vecmag{\vw}=\sqrt{s^2(\cos(t)^2+\sin(t)^2) +s^2}=2s\text{.}\) Thus, Theorem 12.10.4 gives
\begin{equation*} \int_{S_1} f \, dS = \int_1^3 \int_0^{2\pi} (s \cos(t)) \, 2s\, dt\, ds =0\text{.} \end{equation*}
Notice that we can evaluate this integral quickly because the inside integral is \(\int_0^{2\pi} \cos(t)\, dt = 0\text{.}\)
(c)
If we want to calculate the integral of \(f(x,y,z) = x\) over \(S_2\text{,}\) then we have very little extra work to do. Only the bounds on the integral will change since we are using the same parameterization and function from the previous part. We can use the parameterization given by \(\vr(s,t)=\langle s \cos(t), s \sin(t), s \rangle \) with \(1\leq s \leq 3\) and \(-\frac{\pi}{2} \leq t \lt \frac{\pi}{2} \text{.}\) As before, \(\vecmag{\vw} =\sqrt{s^2(\cos(t)^2+\sin(t)^2) +s^2}=2s\text{.}\) Thus, Theorem 12.10.4 gives
\begin{align*} \int_{S_2} f \, dS \amp = \int_1^3 \int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} (s \cos(t)) \, 2s\, dt\, ds\\ \amp = \int_1^3 2s^2 [\sin(t)]_{|_{t=-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}} \, ds\\ \amp = \int_1^3 4s^2 \, ds\\ \amp = \int_1^3 4s^2 \, ds = 104/3\text{.} \end{align*}
It makes sense that the result of \(\int_{S_2} f \, dS\) is positive because the output of our function is non-negative for all points on \(S_2\text{.}\) Remember that the Riemann sum used in calculating surface integrals of scalar functions is the product of the function’s value on each piece times the estimate of the surface area. Thus, if the function’s value is positive for all points on the surface, then the scalar surface integral will also be positive.
