Example 12.10.5.

(a)
For our first part of our example, we will calculuate the scalar integral of \(f(x,y,z)=x\) on the surface \(S_1\) given by \(x^2+y^2=z^2\) such that \(1\leq z \leq 3\text{.}\) This is the same part of the cone used in Example 12.9.8. Without using any calculations or parameterizations, we can reason that this scalar surface integral will be zero. In a scalar surface integral, we are calculating the accumulation of the scalar output over a surface area in space. In Figure 12.10.6, you can see that we can symmetrically break our surface \(S_1\) into two regions that have exactly the same surface area properties and symmetric output values for our scalar function, \(f\text{.}\) The only difference between the red (\(S_3\)) and green (\(S_2\)) of Figure 12.10.6 is that the output of \(f\) will be negative on the red surface \(S_3\) and positive on the green surface \(S_2\text{.}\) No matter how we break up the Riemann sum or the corresponding scalar surface integral for the green side of our surface, applying the same pattern to the red side will give the same result but with an opposite sign. In other words,
\begin{equation*} \int_{S_3} f \enspace dS = - \int_{S_2} f \enspace dS\text{.} \end{equation*}
Therefore,
\begin{equation*} \int_{S_1} f \enspace dS = \int_{S_2} f \enspace dS + \int_{S_3} f \enspace dS = \int_{S_2} f \enspace dS - \int_{S_2} f \enspace dS =0 \end{equation*}
(b)
From a calculation standpoint, we can still verify this result using our parameterization from Example 12.9.8 and Theorem 12.10.4. Specifically, we will use the parameterization of \(S_1\) given by \(\vr(s,t)=\langle s \cos(t), s \sin(t), s \rangle \) with bounds \(1\leq s \leq 3\) and \(0 \leq t \lt 2 \pi\text{.}\) We also know from Example 12.9.8 that
\begin{equation*} \vw= \vr_s \times \vr_t = \langle-s \cos(t), -s \sin(t), s \rangle \end{equation*}
The length of \(\vw\) will be \(\sqrt{s^2(\cos(t)^2+\sin(t)^2) +s^2}=2s\text{.}\) Thus, Theorem 12.10.4 gives
\begin{equation*} \int_{S_1} f \enspace dS = \int_1^3 \int_0^{2\pi} (s \cos(t)) \enspace 2s dt ds =0 \end{equation*}
because the inside integral is \(\int_0^{2\pi} \cos(t) dt = 0\text{.}\)
(c)
If we want to calculate the integral of \(f(x,y,z) = x\) over \(S_2\) given by \(x^2+y^2=z^2\) such that \(1\leq z \leq 3\) and \(0\leq x \text{,}\) then we have very little extra work to do. Only the bounds on the integral will change since we are using the same parameterization and function from the first example. We can use the parameterization given by \(\vr(s,t)=\langle s \cos(t), s \sin(t), s \rangle \) with \(1\leq s \leq 3\) and \(-\frac{\pi}{2} \leq t \lt \frac{\pi}{2} \text{.}\) The length of \(\vw\) will be \(\sqrt{s^2(\cos(t)^2+\sin(t)^2) +s^2}=2s\text{.}\) Thus, Theorem 12.10.4 gives
\begin{align*} \int_{S_2} f \enspace dS \amp = \int_1^3 \int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} (s \cos(t)) \enspace 2s dt ds\\ \amp = \int_1^3 2s^2 [\sin(t)]_{|_{t=-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}} \enspace ds\\ \amp = \int_1^3 4s^2 \enspace ds\\ \amp = \int_1^3 4s^2 = 104/3 \enspace ds \end{align*}
It should make sense to you why the result of \(\int_{S_2} f \enspace dS\) is positive because the output of our function is non-negative for all points on \(S_2\text{.}\) Remember that the Riemann sum used in calculating surface integrals of scalar functions is the product of the function’s output on each piece times the estimate of the surface area. Thus, if the function’s output is positive for all points on the surface, then the scalar surface integral will also be positive.
(d)
For our last example, we will calculate
\begin{equation*} \int_{S_1} z-x^2 \enspace dS \end{equation*}
We can reuse the parameterization and calculations above for \(S_1\) here and skip right to setting up the iterated integral. We will take a moment here to note that converting our scalar function \(g(x,y,z)= z-x^2\) to a function of \(s\) and \(t\) will give \(g(s,t)=s-s^2 \cos(t)^2\text{.}\) Therefore, we get the following iterated integral
\begin{align*} \int_{S_1} z-x^2 \enspace dS \amp = \int_1^3 \int_{0}^{2 \pi} (s-s^2 \cos(t)^2) \enspace 2s dt ds\\ \amp = \int_1^3 2s^2 [t-s(\frac{2t+\sin(2t)}{4})]_{|_{t=0}^{2 \pi}} \enspace ds\\ \amp = \int_1^3 2s^2 (-\pi (s-2)) \enspace ds = -\frac{16\pi}{3} \end{align*}
This result is a bit more difficult to to make sense of as being negative. In Figure 12.10.7 we have a plot of the surface \(S_1\) in blue and the parabolic cylinder surface given by \(z-x^2=0\) is plotted in yellow. The points on the yellow surface correspond to where our scalar function (\(g(x,y,z)= z-x^2\)) gives an output of zero. The points inside the parabolic cylinder have a \(z\)-coordinate greater than \(x^2\) (which means that \(g\) will have a positive output for these points.) The points outside of the parabolic cylinder have a \(z\)-coordinate less than \(x^2\) (which means that \(g\) will have a negative output for these points.) In order to make an argument about whether \(\int_{S_1} z-x^2 \enspace dS\) is positive/negative/zero, we will need to assess whether there is more surface area with a positive output of \(g\text{,}\) more with a negative output, or an equal amount of positive-negative outputs. Actually, our problem is even harder than this. Beacuse the Riemann sum is the product of the output for each piece of our surface times the surface area estimate, we will need to think about which points/pieces have larger or smaller outputs for \(g\text{.}\) This is VERY difficult in general because you will need to a systematic way of estimating both the output of \(g\) and the surface area of different pieces of your surface.
In our case, there is more surface area of \(S_1\) inside the parabolic cylinder than outside. For \((x,y,z)\) points that are far away from the yellow surface, the magnitude of the output of \(g\) for that point will be large. For \((x,y,z)\) points that are close to the yellow surface, thier \(z\)-coordinates will be close to \(x^2\) and thus the output of \(g\) will be close to zero. This means that in order to properly estimate the scalar surface integral, we will need to assess whether there is a greater weighted surface area inside the parabolic cylinder than outside, where the weight of each surface area piece is given by how far the piece is from the yellow surface (the parabolic cylinder surface). This is not an easy argument to make and is not easy to verify visually for this example. For some examples you may be able to make a nice geometric arguement about the size of scalar surface integrals, but the algebraic calculation given by Theorem 12.10.4 gives an exact value.
