We call the quantities \(dx\text{,}\) \(dy\text{,}\) and \(df\) differentials, and we think of them as measuring small changes in the quantities \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(f\text{.}\) Equations (11.5.2) and (11.5.3) express the relationship between these changes. Equation (11.5.3) resembles an important idea from single-variable calculus: when \(y\) depends on \(x\text{,}\) it follows in the notation of differentials that
\begin{equation*} dy = y'~dx = \frac{dy}{dx}~dx. \end{equation*}
