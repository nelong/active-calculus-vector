As we increase \(n\) (the number of pieces of \(C\) in our approximation) and make sure the length of each \(C_i\) goes to zero, the vector field \(\vF\) will be nearly constant on each piece and \(\Delta \mathbf{r}_i \) will be a better approximation of \(C_i\text{.}\) This means that our approximation with \(n\) pieces of \(C\) would be
\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vF(\mathbf{r}_i)\cdot \Delta \mathbf{r}_i \end{equation*}
and the exact amount of work done by \(\vF\) over \(C\) could be calculated by taking the limit of this approximation as the number of pieces increases and the size of each piece goes to zero. This should be a familiar Riemann sum argument from earlier definitions involving integration.
