Fortunately, one familiar class of vector fields can be shown to be path-independent. Let \(f\colon \R^3\to \R\) be a function for which \(\nabla f\) is continuous on a domain \(D\text{.}\) Suppose that \(P\) and \(Q\) are points in \(D\) and let \(C\) be a smooth oriented path from \(P\) to \(Q\text{.}\) Letâ€™s take a look at \(\int_C\nabla f \cdot d\vr\) by fixing an arbitrary parametrization \(\vr(t)\) of \(C\text{,}\) \(a\leq t \leq b\text{.}\) Since we can write the \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) in terms of \(t\text{,}\) the gradient of \(f\) is given by:
\begin{equation*} \nabla f(\vr(t)) = \langle f_x(\vr(t)),f_y(\vr(t)),f_z(\vr(t))\rangle \end{equation*}
So we can write the line integral of the vector field \(\vF = \nabla f\) over \(C\) in the following way:
\begin{equation*} \int_C\nabla f\cdot d\vr = \int_a^b \nabla f(\vr(t))\cdot \vr'(t)\, dt = \int_a^b \langle f_x(\vr(t)), f_y(\vr(t)), f_z(\vr(t))\rangle \cdot \vr'(t)\, dt \end{equation*}
