The function we are integrating in this example gives an output of the x-coordinates of the points on the curve. So we are adding up the \(x\)-values of the points on \(C_1\) and \(C_2\text{,}\) but since the \(x\)-coordinates on \(C_1\) go from -1 to 0 and the \(x\)-coordinates on \(C_2\) go from 0 to 1, you may argue that the values of \(\int_{C_1} x \enspace ds \) and \(\int_{C_2} x \enspace ds \) will cancel out to zero. This is not correct though because the scalar line integrals are not just adding up the output of our scalar function. The Riemann sum used in the scalar line integral is adding up the product of the scalar function’s output multiplied by the displacement of the segment used. Because the displacement used in \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) will not be symmetric, we cannot make the geometric cancelation argument as in Task 12.5.6.a.
