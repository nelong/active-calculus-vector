We first need to consider how we want to represent and measure rotation in three dimensions. When we developed the circulation density on the \(xy\)-plane, we measured the rotational strength at a point \((a,b)\) with an axis of rotation coming out of the \(xy\)-plane. This corresponds to the axis of rotation being given by the blue vector in Figure 12.7.9. Remember that “positive” rotation corresponds to counterclockwise rotation in the \(xy\)-plane. We can generalize this idea to think of rotation in three dimensions as being represented by a vector where

  • the direction of the vector represents the axis of rotation and

  • the magnitude of the vector represents the strength of the rotation.

By convention, we consider positive “rotation” to correspond to counterclockwise rotation when the vector field is viewed looking from the terminal point of the vector to its initial point. In Figure 12.7.10, you can see that each vector corresponds to the rotation displayed and is consistent with the conventions described above.
