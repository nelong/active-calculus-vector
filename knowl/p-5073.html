Before we apply our limit, we will take a moment to simplify our integrals in order to make the limit easier to evaluate. In particular, we will apply the Mean Value Theorem to each of four the integrals above. Applying the Mean Value Theorem to the first integral gives
\begin{equation*} \int_{-h}^{h} F_2(a+t,b+h) dt = (2h) \enspace F_2(t^*_1,b+h)\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(t^*_1\) is some value in the interval \((a-h,a+h)\text{.}\) Applying the Mean Value Theorem to each of the other integrals yields
\begin{gather*} \int_{-h}^{h} F_2(a+t,b+h) dt - \int_{-h}^{h} F_2(a+t,b-h) dt \\ \quad + \int_{-h}^{h} F_1(a+h,b+t) dt -\int_{-h}^{h} F_1(a-h,b+t) dt \\ (2h) \left(F_2(t^*_1,b+h)-F_2(t^*_2,b-h)+F_1(a+h,t^*_3)-F_1(a-h,t^*_1)\right) \end{gather*}
where \(t^*_1,t^*_2\) are values in \((a-h,a+h)\) and \(t^*_3,t^*_4\) are values in \((b-h,b+h)\text{.}\) Thus our flow density can be measured by looking at the limit as \(h\to 0\) of the net flow (in or out) over the square divided by the area of the square.
\begin{gather*} \text{Flow Density}(a,b)=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0} \frac{\text{net flow}}{\text{area}} \\ \quad = \lim_{h\rightarrow 0} \frac{(2h) \left(F_2(t^*_1,b+h)-F_2(t^*_2,b-h)+F_1(a+h,t^*_3)-F_1(a-h,t^*_1)\right)}{4h^2} \end{gather*}
