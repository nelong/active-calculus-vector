The surface \(S_3\) can be parameterized by \(\vr(s,t)=\langle 1,s\cos(t),s\sin(t)\rangle\) where \(s \in [0,1]\) and \(t \in [0,2\pi]\text{.}\) Using TheoremÂ 12.10.4, we need to compute the partial derivatives of \(\vr\) with respect to \(s\) and \(t\text{,}\) then compute \(\vecmag{\vr_s \times \vr_t}\text{.}\) For this parameterization, we have
\begin{align*} \vr_s \amp = \langle 0, \cos(t), \sin(t) \rangle \\ \vr_t \amp = \langle 0, -s\sin(t), s\cos(t) \rangle \\ \vr_s \times \vr_t \amp = \langle s (\cos(t))^2+s (\sin(t))^2, 0, 0 \rangle \text{.} \end{align*}
Simplifying \(\vr_s \times \vr_t\) we have
\begin{equation*} \vr_s \times \vr_t =s \langle 1, 0, 0 \rangle \end{equation*}
and thus \(\vecmag{\vr_s \times \vr_t} = s\text{,}\) with no absolute values required here \(0\leq s\leq 1\text{.}\) This may be a familiar result from a change of coordinates between rectangular coordinates and polar coordinates, \(dA = dx\, dy = r\, dr\, d\theta\text{.}\)
