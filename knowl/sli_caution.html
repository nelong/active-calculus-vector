Example 12.5.16.

For our last example, we will compute the scalar line integral
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_1+C_2} x \enspace ds \end{equation*}
where the paths \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) are given in Figure 12.5.17.
The function we are integrating in this example gives an output of the x-coordinates of the points on the curve. So we are adding up the \(x\)-values of the points on \(C_1\) and \(C_2\text{,}\) but since the \(x\)-coordinates on \(C_1\) go from -1 to 0 and the \(x\)-coordinates on \(C_2\) go from 0 to 1, you may argue that the values of \(\int_{C_1} x \enspace ds \) and \(\int_{C_2} x \enspace ds \) will cancel out to zero. This is not correct though because the scalar line integrals are not just adding up the output of our scalar function. The Riemann sum used in the scalar line integral is adding up the product of the scalar function’s output multiplied by the displacement of the segment used. Because the displacement used in \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) will not be symmetric, we cannot make the geometric cancelation argument as in Task 12.5.6.a.
We will use Theorem 12.5.11 to evaluate our line integral. We parameterize \(C_1\) with \(\vr_1(t)= \langle -1=t,2-2t\rangle\) and parameterize \(C_2\) with \(\vr_2(t) =\langle t,t\rangle \text{.}\) Both parameterizations use parameter values in the interval \([0,1]\text{.}\) We can calculate \(\Vert{\vr_1}\Vert = \sqrt{5}\) and \(\Vert{\vr_2}\Vert = \sqrt{2}\text{.}\) Thus we can evaluate our scalar line integral as
\begin{gather*} \int_{C_1+C_2} x \enspace ds = \int_{C_1} x \enspace ds +\int_{C_2} x \enspace ds\\ \quad = \int_0^1 (-1+t) \sqrt{5} \enspace dt + \int_0^1 (t) \sqrt{2} \enspace dt\\ \quat = -\sqrt{5}+\frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \neq 0 \end{gather*}
In the calculation above, you can see how the difference in displacements manifests differently in the two integrals (as \(\sqrt{5}\) and \(\sqrt{2}\)) which makes the final result not quite cancel out to zero.
