\(\int_{C_1}\vec F\cdot d\vec r = 0\text{,}\) because the curve moves straight up and down, and the vector field points from right to left. By similar logic, we note that both of \(\int_{C2}\vec F\cdot d\vec r\) and \(\int_{C3}\vec F\cdot d\vec r\) are negative. However, because \(C_2\) has a smaller component pointing in the opposite direction as the vector field, the integral on \(C_2\) is larger than that on \(C_3\text{.}\) Applying this logic to each of the curves, we find that
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_{3}}\vec F\cdot\vec r \lt \int_{C_{2}}\vec F\cdot\vec r \lt \int_{C_{1}}\vec F\cdot\vec r. \end{equation*}
