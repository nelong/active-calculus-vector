Solution 12.6.4.1.1.

(a) The parameterization is \(\vec r(t) = (x(t),y(t)) = \langle t, \pi\,t/2\rangle\text{.}\) Then \(\vec r\,'(t) = \langle 1, \pi/2\rangle\text{,}\) so that
\begin{equation*} \int_C\nabla f\cdot d\vec r = \int_0^1 \langle -8xe^{-x^{2}}\sin\!\left(5y\right), 20e^{-x^{2}}\cos\!\left(5y\right)\rangle\cdot \langle 1,\pi/2\rangle\,dt = \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \int_0^1 \left(5\cdot 4\frac{\pi }{2}e^{-t^{2}}\cos\!\left(\frac{5\pi t}{2}\right)-8te^{-t^{2}}\sin\!\left(\frac{5\pi t}{2}\right)\right)\,dt. \end{equation*}
As noted, it's simpler to integrate on the pair of line segments \(C_1\) from \((0,0)\) to \((1,0)\) and \(C_2\) from \((1,0)\) to \((1,\pi/2)\text{.}\) Parameterizing these in a manner similar to our parameterization of \(C\text{,}\) on \(C_1\) we have \(\vec r = \langle t, 0\rangle\) and on \(C_2\) \(\vec r = \langle 1, \pi t/2\rangle\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_1}\nabla f\cdot d\vec r = \int_0^1 0\,dt = 0, \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_2}\nabla f\cdot d\vec r = \int_0^1 \frac{\pi}{2}\,20\,e^{-1}\,\cos(5\frac{\pi}{2}\,t)\,dt = 4 e^{-1}, \end{equation*}
so that the change in \(f\) is \((4 e^{-1}) + (0) = 4 e^{-1}\text{.}\) (b) The other way to find the change in \(f\) between these two points is to first find \(f\text{.}\) To do this, observe that
\begin{equation*} -8xe^{-x^{2}}\sin\!\left(5y\right)\,\vec i + 20e^{-x^{2}}\cos\!\left(5y\right)\,\vec j = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(4e^{-x^{2}}\sin\!\left(5y\right) \right) \vec i + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(4e^{-x^{2}}\sin\!\left(5y\right) \right) \vec j = \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \nabla \left(4e^{-x^{2}}\sin\!\left(5y\right)\right). \end{equation*}
So one possibility for \(f\) is \(f(x,y)=e^{x^2}\sin y\text{.}\) Thus,
\begin{equation*} \mbox{Change in } f\bigg|_{(0,0)}^{(1,\pi/2)} = 4e^{-x^{2}}\sin\!\left(5y\right) \bigg|_{(0,0)}^{(1,\pi/2)}= 4 e^{-1} - 0 = 4 e^{-1}. \end{equation*}
