(a) We see that \(\vec F,\vec G,\vec H\) are all gradient vector fields, since
\begin{equation*} \nabla (3xy) = \vec F\quad\mbox{for all }x,y, \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \nabla (\tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{x}{y}\right)) = \vec G\quad\mbox{except where }y=0, \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \nabla (4\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}) = \vec H\quad\mbox{except at }(0,0). \end{equation*}
Other answers are possible. (b) Parameterizing the unit circle, \(C\text{,}\) by \(x=\cos t\text{,}\) \(y=\sin t\text{,}\) \(0\le t\le 2\pi\text{,}\) we have \(\vec r'(t) =-\sin t\,\vec i+ \cos t\,\vec j\text{,}\) so
\begin{equation*} \int_C\vec F\cdot d\vec r = 3\int_0^{2\pi}((\sin t)\vec i+(\cos t)\vec j)\cdot ((-\sin t)\vec i+(\cos t)\vec j)\,dt = 3\int_0^{2\pi}\cos (2t)\,dt = 0. \end{equation*}
The vector field \(\vec G\) is tangent to the circle, pointing in the opposite direction to the parameterization, and of length 1 everywhere. Thus
\begin{equation*} \int_C\vec G\cdot d\vec r= -1\cdot\hbox{ Length of circle }=-2\pi. \end{equation*}
The vector field \(\vec H\) points radially outward, so it is perpendicular to the circle everywhere. Thus
\begin{equation*} \int_C\vec H\cdot d\vec r = 0. \end{equation*}
(c) Green’s Theorem does not apply to the computation of the line integrals for \(\vec G\) and \(\vec H\) because their domains do not include the origin, which is in the interior, \(R\text{,}\) of the circle. Green’s Theorem does apply to \(\vec F=3y\vec i+3x\vec j\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \int_C\vec F\cdot d\vec r = \int_R\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x} F_2 - \frac{\partial}{\partial y} F_1\right)\,dx\,dy = \int 0\,dx\,dy=0. \end{equation*}
