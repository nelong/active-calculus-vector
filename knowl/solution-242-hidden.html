The surface \(S_3\) can be parameterized by \(\vr(s,t)=\langle 1,s\cos(t),s\sin(t)\rangle\) where \(s \in [0,1]\) and \(t \in [0,2\pi]\text{.}\) Using Theorem 12.10.4, we need to compute the partial derivatives of \(\vr\) with respect to \(s\) and \(t\text{,}\) then compute \(\vecmag{\vr_s \times \vr_t}\text{.}\) For this parameterization, we have
\begin{align*} \vr_s \amp = \langle 0, \cos(t), \sin(t) \rangle \\ \vr_t \amp = \langle 0, -s\sin(t), s\cos(t) \rangle \\ \vr_s \times \vr_t \amp = \langle s (\cos(t))^2+s (\sin(t))^2, 0, 0 \rangle \text{.} \end{align*}
Simplifying \(\vr_s \times \vr_t\) we have
\begin{equation*} \vr_s \times \vr_t =s \langle 1, 0, 0 \rangle \end{equation*}
and thus \(\vecmag{\vr_s \times \vr_t} = s\text{,}\) with no absolute values required here \(0\leq s\leq 1\text{.}\) This may be a familiar result from a change of coordinates between rectangular coordinates and polar coordinates, \(dA = dx\, dy = r\, dr\, d\theta\text{.}\)
We can now use Theorem 12.10.4 to compute the scalar surface integral as
\begin{align*} \iint_{S_3} x+z \, dS \amp = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{1} (1+s\sin(t)) (s) ds dt \\ \amp = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{1} s+s^2\sin(t) ds dt \\ \amp = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{3}\sin(t) dt \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2} (2\pi) + 0 = \pi \text{.} \end{align*}
This result should match your answer for part c of Activity 12.10.2 since the output of the function \(f(x,y,z) = x+z\) equals \(1+z\text{.}\) The values of \(z\) will be symmetric but opposite in sign for the parts of the surface above and below the \(xy\)-plane. Thus the scalar surface integral will be one times the area of \(S_3\) which gives the value \(\pi\text{.}\)
