Solution 10.6.8.4.1.

SOLUTION

(a) Let \(k >0\) be constant. The level curves have equation \(k =\frac{130}{x^{2}+y^{2}+1}\) or \(x^2 + y^2 = \frac{130}{k} - 1\text{.}\) If \(\frac{130}{k} - 1 > 0\text{,}\) these equations represent circles in the \(xy\)- plane.

(b) The largest value of the temperature \(T\) is attained at \((0,0)\text{.}\)

The value of the temperature at this point is \(T(0,0) = \frac{130}{1}=130\)

(c) The greatest increase in temperature at \((3,3)\) is in the direction of the gradient at the point.

We have \(\nabla f (x,y) = \left\lt -\frac{260x}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}+1\right)^{2}},-\frac{260y}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}+1\right)^{2}}\right> \; \Rightarrow \nabla f(3, 3) = \left\langle -{\textstyle\frac{780}{361}}, -{\textstyle\frac{780}{361}}\right \rangle \approx \left\lt -2.16066,-2.16066\right>\)

The maximum value of the directional derivative at \((3, 3)\) is given by the magnitude of the gradient at \((3, 3)\text{:}\) \(\; \left| \nabla f(3, 3) \right| = \sqrt{9.33694}\)

(c) The greatest decrease in temperature at \((3,3)\) is in the opposite direction of the gradient at the point: \(-\nabla f(3, 3) = - \left\lt -2.16066,-2.16066\right>\)

The minimum value of the directional derivative at \((3, 3)\) is given by \(-\left| \nabla f(3, 3) \right| = -\sqrt{9.33694}\)
