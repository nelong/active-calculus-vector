\(-\pi/4\le\theta\le\pi/4\text{.}\) Also, the outer (right) boundary of the region is \(\sqrt{2} = x = r\cos\theta\text{.}\) Hence, \(0\le r\le \sqrt{2}/\cos\theta\text{.}\) The integral becomes
\begin{equation*} \int_0^{\sqrt{2}}\int_{-x}^x\,dy\,dx =\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\int_0^{\sqrt{2}/\!\cos\theta}\,r\,dr\,d\theta. \end{equation*}
Then
\begin{equation*} \int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\int_0^{\sqrt{2}/\!\cos\theta}\,r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\left(\frac{r^2}{2}\bigg|_0^{\sqrt{2}/\!\cos\theta}\right)\,d\theta = \int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{1}{\cos^2\theta}\,d\theta. \end{equation*}
And
\begin{equation*} \int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{1}{\cos^2\theta}\,d\theta =1 \tan\theta\bigg|_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4} = 1\cdot(1 - (-1)) = 2. \end{equation*}
Notice that we can check this answer because the integral gives the area of the shaded triangular region which is \(\frac{1}{2} \cdot \sqrt{2} \cdot (2\sqrt{2}) = 2\text{.}\)
