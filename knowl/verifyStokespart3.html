Task 12.11.6.c.
As we saw in Task 12.11.1.b there is more than one surface that has \(C\) as a boundary. We will calculate the flux integral (the right side of Stokes’ Theorem) for a different surface to help motivate why it will not matter which surface we use (as long as we have the correct orientation and boundary). For this part we will use the surface \(z=x^2-y^2\text{,}\) which will be parameterized by \(\vr(s,t)=\langle s\cos(t),s*\sin(t), s^2 (\cos(t)^2-\sin(t)^2) \rangle \) with \(s \in [0,1]\) and \(t \in [0,2\pi]\text{.}\) Additionally, we will use the trig identity \(\cos(2t)=\cos(t)^2-\sin(t)^2 \) to write the last component of our parameterization as \(s^2 \cos(2t)\text{.}\)
Using our parameterization, we get the following for the partial derivative functions and the corresponding normal vector:
\begin{align*} \vr_s(s,t) \amp =\langle \cos(t), \sin(t),2s\cos(2t)\rangle \\ \vr_r(s,t) \amp =\langle -s\sin(t), s\cos(t),-2s^2\sin(2t)\rangle \\ \vw=\vr_s \times \vr_t \amp =\langle -2s^2\sin(2t)\sin(t)-2s^2\cos(2t)\cos(t) , \\ \amp \quad \quad 2s^2\cos(t)\sin(2t)-2s^2\sin(t)\cos(2t), s \cos(t)^2 + s \sin(t)^2 \rangle \\ \amp = \langle -2s^2 \cos(t), 2s^2 \sin(t), s \rangle \end{align*}
Your algebraic simplifications may vary in the last step; Remember that the algebra may look a bit different but you should be consistent with your work and get the same result in the end.
Now we are ready to set up the flux integral as the following iterated integral:
\begin{equation*} \int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} \langle s\cos(t)-1,1-s\sin(t),0 \rangle \cdot \langle -2s^2 \cos(t), 2s^2 \sin(t), s \rangle \enspace dt ds \end{equation*}
You probably noticed that this integral will look more slightly more complicated than our work in the previous part, but if we are consistent, we should get the same result. Evaluating this dot product, then computing each integral gives:
\begin{gather*} \int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} (-2s^2 \cos(t))(s\cos(t)-1) + (2s^2 \sin(t))(1-s\sin(t)) \enspace dt ds \\ \int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} -2s^3 \cos(t)^2 + 2s^2 \cos(t) + 2s^2 \sin(t) -2s^3\sin(t)^2 \enspace dt ds \\ \int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} -2s^3 + 2s^2 \cos(t) + 2s^2 \sin(t) \enspace dt ds \end{gather*}
Because both sine and cosine will integrate to zero over the interval from zero to \(2\pi\text{,}\) we only need to evaluate
\begin{equation*} 2 \pi \int_0^1 -2s^3 \enspace ds= -\pi \end{equation*}
which gives exactly the same result as our circulation integrate AND our flux integral with the other surface.
