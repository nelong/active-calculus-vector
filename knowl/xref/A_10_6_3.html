Activity 11.7.4.

In this activity we investigate how the gradient is related to the directions of greatest increase and decrease of a function. Let \(f\) be a differentiable function and \(\vu\) a unit vector.
  1. Let \(\theta\) be the angle between \(\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\) and \(\vu\text{.}\) Use the relationship between the dot product and the angle between two vectors to explain why
    \begin{equation} D_{\vu} f(x_0,y_0) = |\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle | \cos(\theta).\tag{11.7.6} \end{equation}
  2. At the point \((x_0,y_0)\text{,}\) the only quantity in EquationÂ (11.7.6) that can change is \(\theta\) (which determines the direction \(\vu\) of travel). Explain why \(\theta = 0\) makes the quantity
    \begin{equation*} |\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle| \cos(\theta) \end{equation*}
    as large as possible.
  3. When \(\theta = 0\text{,}\) in what direction does the unit vector \(\vu\) point relative to \(\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\text{?}\) Why? What does this tell us about the direction of greatest increase of \(f\) at the point \((x_0,y_0)\text{?}\)
  4. In what direction, relative to \(\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\text{,}\) does \(f\) decrease most rapidly at the point \((x_0,y_0)\text{?}\)
  5. State the unit vectors \(\vu\) and \(\vv\) (in terms of \(\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\)) that provide the directions of greatest increase and decrease for the function \(f\) at the point \((x_0,y_0)\text{.}\) What important assumption must we make regarding \(\nabla f(x_0,y_0)\) in order for these vectors to exist?
Solution.
  1. Using the dot product we can see that
    \begin{equation*} D_{\vu} f(x_0,y_0) = \langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle \cdot \vu = |\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle| \cos(\theta)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle\) and \(\vu\text{.}\)
  2. The maximum value of \(D_{\vu} f(x_0,y_0)\) will occur when \(\cos(\theta)\) is as large as possible. This happens when \(\cos(\theta) = 1\) or \(\theta = 0\text{.}\)
  3. When \(\theta = 0\text{,}\) the angle between the vector \(\vu\) and the vector \(\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle\) is 0, so the vector \(\vu\) must have the same direction as \(\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle\text{.}\) In other words, the direction of greatest increase of \(f\) at the point \((x_0,y_0)\) is the direction of the vector \(\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle\text{.}\)
  4. If \(f\) increases most rapidly in the direction of \(\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle\) at the point \((x_0,y_0)\text{,}\) then \(f\) must decrease most rapidly in the opposite direction, or in the direction of \(-\langle f_x(x_0,y_0), f_y(x_0,y_0) \rangle\text{.}\)
  5. The unit vector that provides the direction of greatest increase in \(f\) at the point \((x_0,y_0)\) is \(\vu=\frac{1}{|\nabla f(x_0,y_0)|} \nabla f(x_0,y_0)\) and the unit vector that provides the direction of greatest decrease in \(f\) at the point \((x_0,y_0)\) is \(\vv = -\vu\text{.}\) For either vector to exist, it must be the case that \(\nabla f(x_0,y_0) \neq \langle 0,0 \rangle\text{.}\)
