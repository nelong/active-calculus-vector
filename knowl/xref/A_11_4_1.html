Activity 12.4.3.

Suppose we want to find the area of the bounded region \(D\) between the curves
\begin{equation*} y = 1-x^2 \ \ \ \ \ \text{ and } \ \ \ \ \ y=x-1. \end{equation*}
A picture of this region is shown in FigureÂ 12.4.2.
  1. The volume of a solid with constant height is given by the area of the base times the height. Hence, we may interpret the area of the region \(D\) as the volume of a solid with base \(D\) and of uniform height 1. That is, the area of \(D\) is given by \(\iint_D 1 \, dA\text{.}\) Write an iterated integral whose value is \(\iint_D 1 \, dA\text{.}\) (Hint: Which order of integration might be more efficient? Why?)
  2. Evaluate the iterated integral from (a). What does the result tell you?
Solution.
  1. The double integral
    \begin{equation*} \int \int_D 1 \, dA \end{equation*}
    gives the volume of the solid of uniform height 1 and base area equal to the area of \(D\text{,}\) so this integral also gives the area of \(D\text{.}\) To set up this integral, we need to describe the region \(D\text{.}\) If we integrate with respect to \(y\text{,}\) then the graph of \(y=1-x^2\) forms the top of \(D\) and the graph of \(y=x-1\) forms the bottom. To find the limits on \(x\text{,}\) we need to determine the points of intersections of the two curves. Now \(y=1-x^2\) and \(y=x-1\) intersect when \(x^2+x-2 = (x-1)(x+2) = 0\) or when \(x=-2\) and \(x=1\text{.}\) Therefore, the area of \(D\) is given by the iterated integral
    \begin{equation*} \int \int_D 1 \, dA = \int_{-2}^1 \int_{x-1}^{1-x^2} 1 \, dy \, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
  2. Evaluating the iterated integral gives us
    \begin{align*} \int \int_D 1 \, dA \amp = \int_{-2}^1 \int_{x-1}^{1-x^2} 1 \, dy \, dx\\ \amp = \int_{-2}^1 \left (y\mid_{x-1}^{1-x^2} \right ) \, dx \\ \amp = \int_{-2}^1 \left ( (1-x^2) - (x-1) \right ) \, dx\\ \amp = \int_{-2}^1 2-x-x^2 \, dx\\ \amp = \left[2x-\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{x^3}{3}\right]\mid_{-2}^1\\ \amp = \frac{7}{6} - \left(-\frac{10}{3}\right)\\ \amp = \frac{9}{2}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus the area of \(D\) is \(\frac{9}{2}\) square units.
