Exercise 12.

Consider the function \(g\) defined by \(g(x,y) = \frac{xy}{x^2 + y^2}.\)
  1. What is the domain of \(g\text{?}\)
  2. Evaluate limit of \(g\) at \((0,0)\) along the following paths: \(x = 0\text{,}\) \(y = x\text{,}\) and \(y = 2x\text{.}\)
  3. What can you now say about the value of \(\lim_{(x,y) \to (0,0)} g(x,y)\text{?}\)
  4. Is \(g\) continuous at \((0,0)\text{?}\) Why or why not?
  5. Use appropriate technology to sketch both surface and contour plots of \(g\) near \((0,0)\text{.}\) Write several sentences to say how your plots affirm your findings in (a) - (d).
Solution.
  1. As a rational function, \(g\) is defined everywhere except at the points where the denominator is 0. Since \(x^2 + y^2 = 0\) only when \(x=y=0\text{,}\) the domain of \(g\) is all ordered pairs \((x,y)\) not equal to \((0,0)\text{,}\) or the \(x\)-\(y\) plane with the origin removed.
  2. Along the path \(x=0\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{(x,y) \to (0,0)} g(0,y) = \lim_{y \to 0} \frac{0}{y^2} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Along the path \(y=x\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{(x,y) \to (0,0)} g(x,x) = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x^2}{2x^2} = \frac{1}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Along the path \(y=2x\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{(x,y) \to (0,0)} f(x,2x) = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2x^2}{5x^2} = \frac{2}{5}\text{.} \end{equation*}
  3. The results of the previous part of this problem show that \(g\) has different limits at \((0,0)\) along different paths, so \(g\) does not have a limit at \((0,0)\text{.}\)
  4. Either of the conditions: \(g\) is not defined at \((0,0)\text{,}\) \(g\) does not have a limit at \((0,0)\text{,}\) show that \(g\) is not continuous at \((0,0)\text{.}\)
  5. The surface defined by \(g\) appears to have a tear in it at the origin, indicating that \(g\) is not continuous at \((0,0)\text{.}\)
