Preview Activity 9.4.1.

(a)

For this part, we will consider one dimensional vectors which are denoted \(\vv=\langle v_1 \rangle\text{.}\) Notice that vectors in one dimension will only have one component. Graphically, these vectors will look like an arrow on the number line corresponding to the change described by the component.
For this task, we will use the following vectors:
\begin{equation*} \vv_1=\langle 3 \rangle , \quad \vv_2=\langle -2 \rangle , \quad \vv_3=\langle 1.5 \rangle , \quad \vv_4=\langle -\frac{5}{3} \rangle \end{equation*}
  1. Draw representatives of each of \(\vv_1,\vv_2,\vv_3,\vv_4\) on the number line.
  2. Multiply the component of \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_2\) together and use your result to explain how much do \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_2\) move together.
  3. Multiply the component of \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_3\) together and use your result to explain how much do \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_3\) move together. Be sure to note why the sign of this result is different than for the previous task.
  4. Multiply the component of \(\vv_2\) and \(\vv_4\) together and use your result to explain how much do \(\vv_2\) and \(\vv_4\) move together. Be sure to note why the sign of this result is the same as for the previous task

(b)

Now we will look at some similar ideas for 2D vectors. In particular, we will use the following:
\begin{equation*} \vu_1=\langle 1,2 \rangle , \vu_2=\langle -1,1 \rangle , \vu_3=\langle 2,-1 \rangle , \vu_4=\langle 1,-2 \rangle \end{equation*}
  1. Draw representatives of each of \(\vu_1,\vu_2,\vu_3,\vu_4\) on the plane.
  2. Multiply the first components of \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_2\) and use your ideas from part 9.4.1.a to explain what this value says about how much \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_2\) move together horizontally.
  3. Multiply the second components of \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_2\) and use your ideas from part 9.4.1.a to explain what this value says about how much \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_2\) move together vertically.
  4. Add together the results of the previous two parts and explain how this sum relates to how much \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_2\) move together. You should also look at a plot of \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_2\) starting at the same point.

(c)

  1. Multiply the horizontal and vertical components of \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_3\) separately and use these results to write about how much \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_3\) together horizontally and how much \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_3\) move together vertically.
  2. dd together the two results of the previous task and explain how this sum relates to how much \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_3\) move together. You should also look at a plot of \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_3\) starting at the same point.

(d)

Multiply the horizontal and vertical components of \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_4\) separately then add the two results. Use this number and a plot of \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_3\) starting at the same point to explain how much \(\vu_1\) and \(\vu_4\) move together.
