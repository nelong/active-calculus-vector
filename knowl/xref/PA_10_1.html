  3. Next, consider

  5. Now, let’s examine a function of two variables. Let
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y) = 3 - x - 2y. \end{equation*}

    What does the table suggest about \(\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} f(x,y)\text{?}\)

  7. Finally, consider
    \begin{equation*} g(x,y) = \frac{2xy}{x^2+y^2}, \end{equation*}
    which is not defined at \((0,0)\text{.}\) Complete Table 11.2.7. Round to three decimal places.
    What does this suggest about \(\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} g(x,y)\text{?}\)
  8. Explain how your results are reflected in Figure 11.2.8. Compare this limit to the limit in part (c). How are the results similar and how are they different?
Solution.
  1. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}f(x) = 3 \)
  2. On the graph, we can see that just to the left of \(x = 0\) and just to the right of \(x = 0\text{,}\) the function has output values very very close to 3.
  3. \(\lim_{x\to 0}g(x) \) does not exist
  4. We can see visually that \(\lim_{x\to 0^-}g(x)\ = - 1\) and \(\lim_{x\to 0^+}g(x) = 1\ \text{.}\) Since the limit as \(x\) approaches zero from the left and the limit as \(x\) approaches zero from the right are not the same, \(\lim_{x\to 0}g(x)\ \) does not exist.
  5. \(\displaystyle \lim_{(x,y) \to (0,0)} f(x,y) = 3\ \ \)
  6. The curves represent the output, \(z\text{,}\) of the function. We can see that as \(x\) and \(y\) go from 1.5 to 0, the output value gets closer and closer to \(z = 3\text{.}\)
  7. \(\lim_{(x,y) \to (0,0)} g(x,y)\ \) DNE
  8. The graph on the left shows that as the function approaches an \(x\)-value of 0 and \(y\)-value of 0, the function approaches different output values such as 1 and -1.
