We are familiar with equations of lines in the plane in the form \(y = mx+b\text{,}\) where \(m\) is the slope of the line and \((0,b)\) is the \(y\)-intercept. In this activity, we explore a more flexible way of representing lines that we can use not only in the plane, but in higher dimensions as well.
To begin, consider the line through the point \((2,-1)\) with slope \(\frac{2}{3}\) as shown in FigureÂ 9.6.1.
  1. Suppose we increase \(x\) by 1 from the point \((2,-1)\text{.}\) How does the \(y\)-value change? What is the point on the line with \(x\)-coordinate \(3\text{?}\)
  2. Suppose we decrease \(x\) by 3.25 from the point \((2,-1)\text{.}\) How does the \(y\)-value change? What is the point on the line with \(x\)-coordinate \(-1.25\text{?}\)
  3. Now, suppose we increase \(x\) by some arbitrary value \(3t\) from the point \((2,-1)\text{.}\) How does the \(y\)-value change? What is the point on the line with \(x\)-coordinate \(2+3t\text{?}\)
  4. Observe that the slope of the line is related to any vector whose \(y\)-component divided by the \(x\)-component is the slope of the line. For the line in this exercise, we might use the vector \(\langle 3,2 \rangle\text{,}\) which describes the direction of the line. Explain why the terminal points of the vectors \(\vr(t)\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} \vr(t) = \langle 2,-1 \rangle + \langle 3,2 \rangle t, \end{equation*}
    trace out the graph of the line through the point \((2,-1)\) with slope \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\)
  5. Now we extend this vector approach to \(\R^3\) and consider a second example. Let \(\mathcal{L}\) be the line in \(\R^3\) through the point \((1,0,2)\) in the direction of the vector \(\langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle\text{.}\) Find the coordinates of three distinct points on line \(\mathcal{L}\text{.}\) Explain your thinking.
  6. Find a vector in the form
    \begin{equation*} \vr(t) = \langle x_0, y_0, z_0 \rangle + \langle a,b,c \rangle t \end{equation*}
    whose terminal points trace out the line \(\mathcal{L}\) that is described in (e). That is, you should be able to locate any point on the line by determining a corresponding value of \(t\text{.}\)
Solution.
  1. If a line has a slope of \(\frac{2}{3}\text{,}\) this means that an increase of 3 in the \(x\)-direction results in increase of 2 in the \(y\)-direction. If we scale this to a one-unit rate of change, an increase of 1 unit in the \(x\)-direction results in an increase of \(\frac{2}{3}\) in the \(y\)-direction. From the point \((2, -1)\) to the point with an \(x\)-value of 3, this requires a 1 unit increase in the rate of change in the \(x\)-direction, which results in an increase of \(\frac{2}{3}\\) in the \(y\)-direction. Therefore, the point on the line with an \(x\)-coordinate of \(3\) is \((3, -1 + \frac{2}{3}) = (3, -\frac{1}{3})\text{.}\)
  2. If we decrease \(x\) by \(3.25\) from the point \((2, -1)\) the \(y\)-value will change by \(-3.25 * (\frac{2}{3}) = -\frac{13}{6}\text{.}\) Therefore, the point on the line with an \(x\)-coordinate of \(-1.25\) is \((-1.25, -1 + -\frac{13}{6}) = (-1.25, -\frac{19}{6})\text{.}\)
  3. If we increase \(x\) by some arbitrary value \(3t\) from the point \((2, -1)\text{,}\) the \(y\)-value will change by \(3t * \frac{2}{3} = 2t\text{.}\) The point on the line with \(x\)-coordinate \(2 + 3t\) is \((2 + 3t, -1 +2t)\text{.}\)
  4. The terminal point of the vector \(\langle 2, -1 \rangle\) is the point \((2, -1)\text{.}\) Any vector that is a scalar multiple of \(\langle 3, 2 \rangle\) has a slope of \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\) If we start at the terminal point of \(\langle 2, -1\text{,}\) which is the point \((2, -1)\text{,}\) and add scalar multiples of the vector \(\langle 3, 2 \rangle\text{,}\) the terminal point of these scalar vectors will trace out the graph of the line through the point \((2, -1)\) with a slope of \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\)
  5. To find any point on the line, plug in any \(t\)-value. \(\vr(-1) = \langle 1, 0, 2 \rangle + \langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle * -1 = (-1, 1, -2)\text{.}\) \(\vr(1) = \langle 1, 0, 2 \rangle + \langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle * 1 = (3, -1, 6)\text{.}\) \(\vr(2) = \langle 1, 0, 2 \rangle + \langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle * 2 = (5, -2, 10)\text{.}\)
  6. See part \(e\) above.
