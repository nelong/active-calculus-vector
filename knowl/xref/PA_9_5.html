We are familiar with equations of lines in the \(xy\)-plane in the form \(y = mx+b\text{,}\) where \(m\) is the slope of the line and \((0,b)\) is the \(y\)-intercept. In this activity, we explore a more flexible way of representing lines that we can use in the \(xy\)-plane and higher dimensions. To begin, consider the line through the point \((2,-1)\) with slope \(\frac{2}{3}\) as shown in Figure 9.6.2.

(a)

Suppose we increase \(x\) by 1 from the point \((2,-1)\text{.}\) How does the \(y\)-value change? What is the point on the line with \(x\)-coordinate \(3\text{?}\)
Solution.
If a line has a slope of \(\frac{2}{3}\text{,}\) this means that an increase of 3 in the \(x\)-direction results in increase of 2 in the \(y\)-direction. If we scale this to a one-unit rate of change, an increase of 1 unit in the \(x\)-direction results in an increase of \(\frac{2}{3}\) in the \(y\)-direction. From the point \((2, -1)\) to the point with an \(x\)-value of 3, this requires a 1 unit increase in the rate of change in the \(x\)-direction, which results in an increase of \(\frac{2}{3}\) in the \(y\)-direction. Therefore, the point on the line with an \(x\)-coordinate of \(3\) is \((3, -1 + \frac{2}{3}) = (3, -\frac{1}{3})\text{.}\)

(b)

Suppose we decrease \(x\) by 3.25 from the point \((2,-1)\text{.}\) How does the \(y\)-value change? What is the point on the line with \(x\)-coordinate \(-1.25\text{?}\)
Solution.
If we decrease \(x\) by \(3.25\) from the point \((2, -1)\) the \(y\)-value will change by \(-3.25 (\frac{2}{3}) = -\frac{13}{6}\text{.}\) Therefore, the point on the line with an \(x\)-coordinate of \(-1.25\) is \((-1.25, -1 + -\frac{13}{6}) = (-1.25, -\frac{19}{6})\text{.}\)

(c)

Now, suppose we increase \(x\) by some arbitrary value \(3t\) from the point \((2,-1)\text{.}\) How does the \(y\)-value change? What is the point on the line with \(x\)-coordinate \(2+3t\text{?}\)
Solution.
If we increase \(x\) by some arbitrary value \(3t\) from the point \((2, -1)\text{,}\) the \(y\)-value will change by \(3t * \frac{2}{3} = 2t\text{.}\) The point on the line with \(x\)-coordinate \(2 + 3t\) is \((2 + 3t, -1 +2t)\text{.}\)

(d)

Remember that the horizontal component of a vector describes the “run” and the vertical component measures the “rise” of the vector. So the slope of the line is related to any vector whose \(y\)-component divided by the \(x\)-component is the slope of the line. For the line in this activity, we might use the vector \(\langle 3,2 \rangle\) to describe the direction of the line. Explain why the terminal points of the vectors \(\vr(t)\text{,}\) where
\begin{equation*} \vr(t) = \langle 2,-1 \rangle + \langle 3,2 \rangle t, \end{equation*}
trace out the graph of the line through the point \((2,-1)\) with slope \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\)
Solution.
The terminal point of the vector \(\langle 2, -1 \rangle\) is the point \((2, -1)\text{.}\) Any vector that is a scalar multiple of \(\langle 3, 2 \rangle\) has a slope of \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\) If we start at the terminal point of \(\langle 2, -1\text{,}\) which is the point \((2, -1)\text{,}\) and add scalar multiples of the vector \(\langle 3, 2 \rangle\text{,}\) the terminal point of these scalar vectors will trace out the graph of the line through the point \((2, -1)\) with a slope of \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\)

(e)

Now we extend this vector approach to \(\R^3\) and consider a second example. Let \(\mathcal{L}\) be the line in \(\R^3\) through the point \((1,0,2)\) in the direction of the vector \(\langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle\text{.}\) Find the coordinates of three distinct points on line \(\mathcal{L}\text{.}\) Explain your thinking.
Solution.
To find any point on the line, plug in any \(t\)-value. \(\vr(-1) = \langle 1, 0, 2 \rangle + (-1) \langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle = (-1, 1, -2)\text{.}\) \(\vr(1) = \langle 1, 0, 2 \rangle + 1 \langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle = (3, -1, 6)\text{.}\) \(\vr(2) = \langle 1, 0, 2 \rangle + 2\langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle = (5, -2, 10)\text{.}\)

(f)

Find a vector in the form
\begin{equation*} \vr(t) = \langle x_0, y_0, z_0 \rangle + \langle a,b,c \rangle t \end{equation*}
whose terminal points trace out the line \(\mathcal{L}\) that is described in (e). That is, you should be able to locate any point on the line by determining a corresponding value of \(t\text{.}\)
Solution.
\(\vr(t) =\langle 1, 0, 2 \rangle + t \langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle \)
