Preview Activity 13.3.1.

Let \(\vF=\langle xy,y^2\rangle\text{,}\) let \(C_1\) be the line segment from \((1,1)\) to \((4,1)\text{,}\) let \(C_2\) be the line segment from \((4,1)\) to \((4,3)\text{,}\) and let \(C_3\) be the line segment from \((1,1)\) to \((4,3)\text{.}\) Also let \(C = C_1 + C_2\text{.}\) This vector field and the curves are shown in FigureÂ 13.3.1.

(a)

Every point along \(C_1\) has \(y=1\text{.}\) Therefore, along \(C_1\text{,}\) the vector field \(\vF\) can be viewed purely as a function of \(x\text{.}\) In particular, along \(C_1\text{,}\) we have \(\vF(x,1) = \langle x,1\rangle\text{.}\) Since every point along \(C_2\) has the same \(x\)-value, write \(\vF\) as a function of \(y\) only (for the points on \(C_2\)).
Solution.
\(\vF=\langle 4y,y^2\rangle \) along \(C_2\text{.}\)

(b)

Recall that \(d\vr \approx \Delta \vr\text{,}\) and along \(C_1\text{,}\) we have that \(\Delta\vr = \Delta x\vi \approx dx\vi\text{.}\) Thus, \(d\vr = \langle dx,0\rangle\text{.}\) We know that along \(C_1\text{,}\) \(\vF = \langle x,1\rangle\text{.}\)
(i)
Write \(\vF\cdot d\vr\) along \(C_1\) without using a dot product.
Solution.
Along the path \(C_1\text{,}\) \(\vF \cdot d\vr=\langle x,1 \rangle \cdot \langle dx, 0\rangle=x dx\text{.}\)
(ii)
What interval of \(x\)-values describes \(C_1\text{?}\)
Solution.
Since \(C_1\) is the line segment from \((1,1)\) to \((4,1)\text{,}\) we have that \(1\leq x\leq 4\text{.}\)
(iii)
Write \(\int_{C_1} \vF\cdot d\vr\) as an integral of the form \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\) and evaluate the integral.
Solution.
From the previous parts,
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_1} \vF\cdot d\vr=\int_1^4 x dx = 8-\frac{1}{2} = \frac{15}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

(c)

Use an analogous approach to write \(\int_{C_2} \vF\cdot d\vr\) as an integral of the form \(\int_c^d g(y)\, dy\) and evaluate the integral.
Solution.
Along \(C_2\text{,}\) \(d\vr = \langle 0,dy\rangle\) and \(1\leq y\leq 3\text{.}\) Thus, we have
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_2} \vF\cdot d\vr = \int_1^3 y^2 dy = 9-\frac{1}{3} = \frac{26}{3}\text{.} \end{equation*}

(d)

Use the previous parts and a property of line integrals to calculate \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr\) without having to evaluate any additional integrals.
Solution.
\begin{equation*} \int_{C} \vF\cdot d\vr =\int_{C_1} \vF\cdot d\vr+\int_{C_2} \vF\cdot d\vr= \frac{15}{2}+\frac{26}{3}=\frac{97}{6} \end{equation*}
