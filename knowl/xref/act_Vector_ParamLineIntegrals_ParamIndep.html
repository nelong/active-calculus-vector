Activity 13.3.5.

(a)
The typical parametrization of the line segment from \((0,1)\) to \((3,3)\) (the oriented curve \(C_3\) in Example 13.3.9) is \(\vr(t) = \langle 3t,1+2t\rangle\) where \(0 \leq t\leq 1\text{.}\) Use this parametrization to calculate \(\int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\) for the vector field \(\vF = x\vi\) and compare your answer to the result of Example 13.3.9.
Solution.
Using the parametrization given the line integral becomes
\begin{equation*} \int_0^{1} \langle 3t,0\rangle \cdot \langle 3,2 \rangle \enspace dt = \int_0^1 9t \, dt = \frac{9}{2} \end{equation*}
This is the same result as in Example 13.3.9.
(b)
Calculate \(\int_C \langle (3xy+e^z), x^2, (4z+xe^z)\rangle\cdot d\vr\) where \(C\) is the oriented curve consisting of the line segment from the origin to \((1,1,1)\) followed by the line segment from \((1,1,1)\) to \((0,0,2)\text{.}\)
Solution.
Let \(\vr_1(t) = \langle t,t,t \rangle\) where\(0 \leq t \leq 1\) and \(\vr_2(t) = \langle 1-t,1-t,1+t \rangle\) where\(0 \leq t \leq 1\text{.}\) Our line integral can be computed with
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_1} \vF \cdot d\vr = \int_0^1 \langle 3t^2+e^t,t^2,4t+te^t\rangle \cdot \langle 1,1,1\rangle \enspace dt= e+\frac{10}{3} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_2} \vF \cdot d\vr = \\ \int_0^1 \langle 3(1-t)^2+e^{1-t},(1-t)^2,4(1+t)+(1-t)e^{1+t} \rangle \cdot \langle -1,-1,1\rangle \enspace dt \\ = 2e^2-3e+\frac{14}{3} \end{equation*}
Thus the line integral over \(C_1\) then \(C_2\) is \(2e^2-2e+8\)
(c)
Calculate \(\int_{C'} \langle 3xy+e^z, x^2, 4z+xe^z\rangle\cdot d\vr\) where \(C_3\) is the line segment from \((0,0,0)\) to \((0,0,2)\text{.}\)
Solution.
The line segment from \((0,0,0)\) to \((0,0,2)\) can be given by \(\vr(t) = \langle 0,0,t \rangle\) with \(0\leq t\leq 2\text{.}\) Note that \(d\vr(t)= \langle 0,0,1 \rangle dt\text{,}\) so
\begin{equation*} \int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr = \int_0^2 4t\enspace dt = 16 \end{equation*}
(d)
Is the vector field you considered in the previous two parts a gradient vector field? Why or why not? How does this compare to the vector field \(\vF\) of Activity 13.3.4?
Solution.
No. One way to observe this would be to check the partial derivative with respect to \(y\) of the \(\vi\) component and compare it to the partial derivative with respect to \(x\) of the \(\vj\) component. Since they are not equal, Clairaut’s Theorem tells us that this vector field is not a gradient vector field.
